# Hebrew — native-copy reference

Language-specific rules for rewriting Hebrew copy to native Israeli register. Read this
before rewriting Hebrew. Contents:

1. Address strategy (gender) — the core decision
2. De-calque rules — killing the English skeleton
3. Register control — professional-warm vs casual
4. Numbers, punctuation, RTL
5. Hebrew tells checklist
6. Worked before/after (from a real landing)

## 1. Address strategy (gender) — the core decision

Hebrew forces a gender choice in every direct address. There is no neutral "you." Pick one
strategy for the whole piece and apply it everywhere. For a mixed-gender business audience
(the common case: owners who may be men or women), the goal is that no reader feels the copy
was written for someone else.

**The trap:** translation defaults to masculine singular `אתה` + masculine verb forms
(`אתה מנהל`, `כשאתה עסוק`, `בשבילך`). Half the audience is now reading copy addressed to a man.

**Two primary strategies. Pick ONE for the piece by the voice you want, then apply it everywhere.**

**Strategy A — Singular homograph-neutral (keeps the intimate "you").** This is often the
best choice when the source (English/Spanish) speaks to one reader directly and you want to
keep that 1:1 warmth. Exploit the fact that many second-person forms are spelled *identically*
for masculine and feminine in unvocalized Hebrew — the reader supplies their own gender. Build
the direct address almost entirely from these homographs and from infinitive/impersonal verbs,
and never commit to a gendered conjugated verb (`אתה עסוק` / `את עסוקה`).

Gender-homograph forms (same consonants for m. and f., so safe in normal text):
`שלך` (your), `לך` (to/for you), `אותך` (you, obj.), `בשבילך` (for you), `אתך` (with you),
`יש לך` (you have), and 2nd-person past verbs like `ישנת` (you slept), `בחרת` (you chose).

- `אתה מנהל את העסק` → `יש לך עסק לנהל` (you have a business to run — homograph + infinitive)
- `כשאתה עסוק` → `כשאין לך רגע פנוי` (when you don't have a free moment)
- `אתה מפסיק לחיות בתוך הטלפון` → `וככה מפסיקים לחיות בתוך הטלפון` (impersonal)
- Keep `שלך`, `לך`, `אותך`, `בשבילך` as-is — they are already gender-safe.

**Strategy B — Plural address (`לשון רבים`).** Address the reader in the plural: `אתם`,
`שלכם`, `אתכם`, plural imperatives `כתבו / צפו / גלו / קבעו`. Modern Israeli marketing treats
the plural as the unmarked, inclusive form — everyone reads `אתם` as "all of you," not "you
men." Warm and energetic, but it shifts the voice from intimate 1:1 to "you all," a bigger
change from a singular source. Choose it for punchy, collective, campaign-style copy.
- `אתה מנהל את העסק` → `אתם מנהלים את העסק`
- `כשאתה עסוק` → `כשאתם עסוקים`
- `בשבילך` → `בשבילכם`

**How to choose:** if the English/Spanish speaks to one owner and the brand is intimate/personal
(most service-business landings), prefer **Strategy A** — it preserves the voice and reads
native without the "you all" shift. If the copy is a collective rallying call or the brand voice
is already plural, use **Strategy B**. Both are fully gender-safe; the difference is voice, not
correctness.

**Supporting moves (use within either strategy):**

- **Make the business or product the subject.** Let "your business" / "your AI" act, sidestepping
  address entirely. `ה-AI שלך עונה במקומך` (your AI answers for you) — `שלך`/`במקומך` are homographs.
- **Infinitive / impersonal for statements.** `אתה יכול` → `אפשר` (it's possible);
  `אתה רואה` → `רואים` / `עד שרואים`.
- **Nominalization.** `אתה כבר לא מפספס הודעות` → `סוף להודעות שמתפספסות` (an end to missed messages).

**What to avoid:**
- **Do not** use the `אתה/את` slashed double-gender form (`מנהל/ת`, `שלך/שלכם`) in marketing
  hero/body copy — it is bureaucratic form-speak, not brand voice. It is acceptable only in
  legal/administrative microcopy where nothing else fits.
- **Do not** mix strategies by accident. If CTAs are plural imperatives (`כתבו`, `צפו`) but
  body copy is singular `אתה`, unify — bring the body into the plural. A deliberate split
  (all buttons imperative-plural, all body impersonal) is fine; an accidental one is a tell.
- Feminine plural (`אתן`) is not used for mixed marketing audiences; plural masculine is the
  inclusive default.

**State your choice.** e.g. "Strategy: plural address throughout; CTAs as plural imperatives;
statements reframed to the business/AI where it reads stronger." Let the user veto before you
rewrite everything.

## 2. De-calque rules — killing the English skeleton

Words can be Hebrew while the sentence is still English underneath. Rebuild the sentence the
way Hebrew builds it.

- **Reframe, don't transliterate abstractions.** English marketing abstractions ("solutions,"
  "empower," "streamline," "unlock your potential") have no native Hebrew equivalent that
  isn't stiff. Say the concrete thing. `פתרון אוטומציה חכם` (a smart automation solution) →
  name what it does: `עונה בוואטסאפ במקומכם`.
- **CTAs the Israeli way.** Native Israeli CTAs are short, direct, plural imperatives:
  `דברו איתנו`, `קבעו דמו`, `שלחו הודעה`, `בואו נתחיל`. Avoid translated politeness like
  `אנו מזמינים אותך ל...` (we invite you to) — it reads formal and foreign.
- **`עבור` vs `בשביל`.** `עבור` (for) is correct but formal/written; in warm marketing
  `בשביל` / `בשבילכם` usually reads more native. `בנוי עבורך` → `בנוי בשבילכם`.
- **Drop over-formal connectors.** `על מנת` → `כדי`; `באמצעות` → `עם` / `דרך` where it flows;
  `בנוסף לכך` → `וגם`. Formal register is a common translation artifact.
- **Word order.** Hebrew is comfortable leading with the verb or the object for emphasis.
  Don't preserve English subject-first order just because the source had it.

## 3. Register control — professional-warm vs casual

Two registers; pick by audience, default to the first.

- **Professional-warm (default, for business owners / services).** Clear, direct, human, a
  little energetic. Plural address. No corporate stiffness, no street slang. This is what
  sells to a salon owner, a clinic, a restaurant.
- **Casual (for conversational bot/voice personas or youth brands).** Lighter, may use mild
  colloquialisms (`בא לכם`, `אין בעיה`, `יאללה` sparingly). Only when the brand is genuinely
  casual.

**Slang caution.** Heavy slang (`אחלה`, `סבבה`, `וואלה`, `פיצוץ`) does not equal "native" —
to a business-owner audience it usually reads *less* professional. Native and slangy are
different axes. Reach for warmth through directness and rhythm, not slang.

## 4. Numbers, punctuation, RTL

- **Numerals:** use Western Arabic numerals (2, 8–15, 250) — standard in Israeli text. Do not
  spell out unless the source did for rhythm.
- **Currency:** `₪` or `ש"ח`; conventionally after the number with a space: `250 ₪` / `250 ש"ח`.
- **Ranges:** `8–15` reads fine; in RTL the range still means low-to-high.
- **Geresh/gershayim:** acronyms and initialisms take gershayim: `ש"ח`, `בע"מ`, and a single
  quote (geresh) for single letters. Latin-script brand/product tokens (`AI`, `WhatsApp`,
  `CRM`, `Kadmon`) stay Latin and are fine embedded in RTL text.
- **RTL punctuation:** ensure sentence-final punctuation and `<br>`/`<span>` boundaries wrap
  the Hebrew correctly; when editing HTML i18n strings, keep the existing tags/entities
  (`<br />`, `<span class="...">`, `&amp;`) exactly and only change the Hebrew text between them.
- **Mixed direction:** a Latin token inside Hebrew (e.g. `בוואטסאפ` vs `ב-WhatsApp`) — prefer
  the transliterated Hebrew form (`וואטסאפ`) for flow when the brand allows; keep Latin for
  formal product names.

## 5. Hebrew tells checklist (hunt for these)

- Masculine-default `אתה` / masculine verb forms in copy for a mixed audience.
- Inconsistent address (singular body + plural buttons).
- Slashed `מנהל/ת`-style double-gender in brand copy.
- Formal translation connectors: `על מנת`, `באמצעות`, `אנו מזמינים אותך`, `עבורך`.
- Abstraction calques: `פתרונות`, `להעצים`, `לייעל` used where a concrete verb belongs.
- Agreement slips: subject/verb or noun/adjective gender-number mismatch (e.g.
  `כל הודעה מגיע לה מענה` → `כל הודעה מקבלת מענה`).
- English word order preserved under Hebrew words.

## 6. Worked before/after (real landing strings)

**Gender fix (statement) — both strategies:**
Before: `אתה מנהל את העסק. הטלפון מנהל אותך.`
- Strategy A (homograph-neutral, keeps intimate "you"): `יש לך עסק לנהל. אבל הטלפון מנהל אותך.`
- Strategy B (plural): `אתם מנהלים את העסק. הטלפון מנהל אתכם.`
Why: A pivots on `לנהל`/`מנהל` and rides the homographs `יש לך`/`אותך`, keeping the intimate
1:1 punch of the source; B is the collective version. Both are gender-safe — pick by voice.

**Gender fix (card body):**
Before: `ההודעה נכנסת כשאתה עסוק. עד שאתה רואה אותה, הם כבר קבעו במקום אחר.`
- Strategy A: `ההודעה נכנסת כשאין לך רגע פנוי. עד שרואים אותה, הם כבר קבעו במקום אחר.`
- Strategy B: `ההודעה נכנסת כשאתם עסוקים. עד שאתם רואים אותה, הם כבר קבעו במקום אחר.`
Why: A swaps `כשאתה עסוק` for the homograph `כשאין לך רגע פנוי` and impersonal `עד שרואים`;
B goes plural. Keep whichever matches the strategy chosen for the whole page.

**Reframe to the product (drops gender + stronger):**
Before: `אתה מפסיק לחיות בתוך הטלפון.`
After: `מפסיקים לחיות בתוך הטלפון.` — or, stronger: `ה-AI שלכם מטפל בזה. אתם יוצאים מהטלפון.`
Why: impersonal/plural removes the gender assumption and reads more native.

**Agreement + de-calque:**
Before: `כל הודעה מגיע לה מענה`
After: `כל הודעה מקבלת מענה`
Why: fixes the number/gender agreement and reads cleaner.

**De-calque the eyebrow:**
Before: `AI לעסק, בנוי עבורך`
After: `AI משלכם, בנוי לעסק שלכם`
Why: `עבורך` (formal, masculine) → warm plural possessive; reframes to "your own AI."

**CTA (already native — keep):**
`כתבו לנו בוואטסאפ`, `צפו בדמו של 2 דקות` — plural imperatives, correct. Use these as the
consistency target: bring the singular body copy up to match this register.
